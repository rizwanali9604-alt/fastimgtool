How to Compress an Image for Email in 2026

Attachment limits for Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo — and how to shrink any photo to fit, without making it look bad.

"The attachment is too large to send." Modern phone cameras produce 5–12 MB photos, so it only takes two or three of them to blow past your email provider's limit. The good news: a photo only needs to look good on a screen, not on a billboard — which means you can shrink it by 80–95% and the recipient won't notice a thing. Here's how to do it in seconds.

Email attachment size limits (2026)

Email service Max attachment size
Gmail25 MB (sends as Drive link above that)
Outlook.com20 MB
Yahoo Mail25 MB
iCloud Mail20 MB
Most corporate / Exchange10 MB (often stricter)

These caps cover all attachments combined, and encoding adds roughly 33% overhead. The practical takeaway: keep your total attachments under about 20 MB, and if you're emailing a business or government address, assume a 10 MB ceiling.

How to compress an image for email

The free FastImageTool Image Compressor runs in your browser, so your files are never uploaded to a server:

  1. Open the Image Compressor and drag in your photo.
  2. Set quality to about 70–80%. This is the sweet spot where the file shrinks dramatically but stays sharp on screen.
  3. Reduce the dimensions to 1600–2048 px on the longest side if the recipient only needs to view it (not print it).
  4. Check the new size shown by the tool — most phone photos drop from several MB to a few hundred KB.
  5. Download and attach. Repeat for each photo, or send in batches if the total is still large.

What file size should you aim for?

  • Viewing on screen: 200–500 KB per photo is plenty and looks crisp on any monitor or phone.
  • Light printing (up to A4): 1–2 MB at full resolution keeps detail.
  • Many photos in one email: target ~300 KB each so you can fit 20–30 under a 25 MB limit.

Which format to send

  • JPEG — best for photos; smallest files. Use this 90% of the time.
  • PNG — only for screenshots, logos, or anything needing transparency or perfectly sharp text. PNGs are larger, so compress or convert photos to PNG → JPG if size matters.
  • WebP — even smaller than JPEG and fine for most modern recipients, though JPEG is the safest universal choice.

Common mistakes

  • Sending straight from the camera roll: full-resolution photos waste the recipient's bandwidth and hit limits fast.
  • Compressing the only copy: always keep your original as a master and email a compressed duplicate.
  • Over-compressing: below ~50% quality you'll see blocky artifacts. Stay in the 70–85% range.
  • Zipping photos: images are already compressed, so a ZIP barely shrinks them — compress the images themselves instead.

Frequently asked questions

What is the email attachment size limit?

Gmail and Yahoo allow 25 MB, Outlook.com and iCloud 20 MB, and many corporate systems just 10 MB. Limits cover all attachments combined, so aim to stay under ~20 MB.

How do I reduce the file size of a photo to email it?

Open it in the Image Compressor, set quality to 70–80%, optionally reduce dimensions to 1600–2048 px, and download. A 5–8 MB photo becomes a few hundred KB.

What is the best format for emailing images?

JPEG for photos (smallest), PNG only for transparency or sharp graphics, WebP for even smaller files with modern recipients.

How do I email a lot of photos at once?

Compress each one first; if the total still exceeds the limit, split across emails or share a cloud link.

Does compressing for email ruin the photo?

No — at 70–85% quality the difference is invisible on screen. Keep your original as a master.
